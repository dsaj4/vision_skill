Proceeding with explicit assumptions: early-stage MVP, small scope, likely built by one person or a very small team, aimed at students who need practical interview practice rather than full career-services software.

**Step 1**
`SWOT factor identification` for a small interview-prep tool for university students

| Strengths (S) | Weaknesses (W) |
|---|---|
| Narrow audience makes the product easier to position and design. | Students often have low willingness to pay. |
| Clear pain point: many students lack interview practice and structured answers. | Trust is hard at the start; bad feedback quality will hurt retention fast. |
| Small MVP is feasible: question bank, mock practice, answer feedback, progress tracking. | Student needs vary a lot by major, country, and job type. |
| Easier acquisition through campuses, clubs, and career communities. | Limited real hiring data and domain depth compared with mature career platforms. |
| The tool can show visible short-term value before an interview. | Seasonality: usage may spike around recruiting cycles and drop afterward. |

| Opportunities (O) | Threats (T) |
|---|---|
| Universities still under-serve personalized, on-demand mock interview prep. | Large AI assistants and career platforms can copy basic interview coaching features. |
| Students are comfortable using AI for resume and interview help. | Generic AI tools may feel “good enough” for free. |
| Role-specific prep for internships, graduate jobs, consulting, finance, tech, etc. can differentiate the product. | If feedback is inaccurate or overconfident, user trust can collapse quickly. |
| Partnerships with career centers, student orgs, bootcamps, and clubs could lower acquisition cost. | Privacy concerns around storing interview answers, recordings, or personal data. |
| A lightweight tool can expand later into resumes, cover letters, and application tracking. | Competition for student attention is intense; retention can be weak without a near-term interview need. |

My read: the strongest starting logic is `clear problem + narrow user + feasible MVP`, while the main risk is `being too generic and too easy to replace`.
